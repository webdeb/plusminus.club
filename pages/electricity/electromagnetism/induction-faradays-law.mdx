### Electromagnetic Induction and Faraday's Law

Electromagnetic induction is a phenomenon that underlies the operation of various electrical devices, including generators and transformers. It involves the production of an electromotive force (EMF) or voltage in a conductor when it is exposed to a changing magnetic field. Faraday's Law of electromagnetic induction provides a quantitative relationship between the rate of change of magnetic flux and the induced EMF. In this section, we will explore the concept of electromagnetic induction and Faraday's Law in detail.

#### Faraday's Experiments

The understanding of electromagnetic induction originated from the experimental work conducted by Michael Faraday in the 19th century. Faraday's experiments demonstrated the relationship between magnetic fields and induced currents. #### Magnetic Flux and Faraday's Law

Magnetic flux is a measure of the magnetic field passing through a given area. Faraday's Law states that the induced electromotive force (EMF) in a circuit is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux. We will explore the concept of magnetic flux, its calculation, and the mathematical expression of Faraday's Law in this subsection.

#### Lenz's Law and Induced Currents

Lenz's Law, formulated by Heinrich Lenz, provides a directionality aspect to Faraday's Law. It states that the direction of an induced current in a conductor is such that it opposes the change in magnetic field causing it. Understanding Lenz's Law is crucial in predicting the direction of induced currents and their impact on the overall circuit.

#### Self-Induction and Inductance

When a changing current flows through a coil or a solenoid, it induces an electromotive force in the same coil, resulting in self-induction. The property of an electric circuit that resists changes in current is called inductance.
